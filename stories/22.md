The ritual circle is drawn in the center of the grotto, marked with salt and ash and symbols you're beginning to remember. Angie kneels at one edge, and you mirror her at the other.

"The ritual has three parts," she says, voice steady and ceremonial. "Recognition. Release. Return. We'll start with recognition."

She places a handful of snow in the center of the circle. It doesn't melt. Instead, it hovers, suspended by something older than either of you.

The snow pauses midair, shaping itself into a heart of frost and glow.

You stare at the shape, breath catching. "What is that?" you whisper.

Angie's eyes are soft. "It's what we've always been," she says. "Even when they tried to keep us apart. Even when they called it wrong or reckless or forbidden. This is what we built in the spaces they couldn't see."

The heart pulses gently, light flickering through the ice. It's fragile and fierce, delicate and enduring.

A memory unfolds: the first time you understood what love meant. Not the grand, burning kind. Not the love that demands and consumes. But the quiet kind. The kind that asks for nothing but presence. The kind that says I will hold you, even when the world says I shouldn't. The love that wasn't romantic, in and of itself, but whose steadiness and realness is perhaps the most romantic thing.

"I loved you," you say, the words simple and devastating. "I still do."

Angie's eyes glisten. "I know," she whispers. "I've always known. Even when you forgot, I knew."
